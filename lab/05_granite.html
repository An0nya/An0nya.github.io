§2Finding 1 — capability is a few flipped bugs, not a risen tide

This is the reframe that makes the rest legible. Granite's no-CoT arithmetic baseline doesn't fail randomly; it fails deterministically, in a handful of specific, reproducible ways. 907 − 131 (= 776) never comes back right across 15 seeds — it settles into one of two stable wrong answers, 676 (9/15, a hundreds-column slip) or 131 (6/15, echoing the subtrahend): 0 of 15 correct, two error basins. A particular carry-drop on an addition probe, likewise. I call these metastable bugs — obs the 0/15 reproducibility is what's measured; inf "basin / attractor" is the interpretation.

A "winning" surgery doesn't lift a diffuse competence — it flips a few of these specific bugs to 12/12 right. And here's the measurement trap: a balanced 49-probe average dilutes a concentrated flip until it looks like a tiny effect. The small balanced t-stats badly understate what's happening — and that's correct behaviour, not a measurement failure. The win really is concentrated. The right lens is per-probe: which exact bug got fixed, and how many configs reach it.

Boarded live monitor.py::swing_probes() emits one row per metastable probe, naming its single best fixer. Probe metastability is itself bimodal: across the 14 swing probes the split is clean — 9 fixed by ≤21 configs, 5 by ≥87, nothing between. The rare-fix probes (e.g. sub_d3_00, 7 configs; mul_2x1_02, 3) are the genuine disjoint specialists — and they're what makes constructive composition possible.

§3Finding 2 — separated treatments stack; proximate ones mask

Once you know wins are disjoint bug-fixes, composition has an apparent rule and a non-obvious failure mode. obs depth-separated treatments tend to stack; same-region or redundant ones mask. I'm calling this a heuristic, not a law — earning the stronger word would take a regression of effect against layer-distance plus an effect-size distribution, and the margins below are thin.

The recipe for a constructive profile: compose per-layer treatments that are (a) depth-separated AND (b) fix disjoint probes — and the per-probe autopsy (Finding 1) is exactly what tells you which fix which. This generalizes the Gemma cross-zone superadditivity (writer-zone × consumer-block) and, crucially, adds its failure mode, which Gemma never made explicit: redundancy near in depth appears to dilute rather than compound. The emerging data-driven math profile that falls out: L5↑(α1.3) + L26↓(prune or α0.15) + L14↑(α1.8), all depth-separated, predicted to stack. §4Finding 3 — the 3-point map lies; non-monotone layers need the fine grid

The published page reads layers through a prune/dup endpoint map. That skeleton is blind to any layer with a U-shaped α-response — and Granite has them. L6 peaks at α≈0.7 (+0.062 Δ-vs-baseline on the gcore in-band probes, n=348/point — the top single-site math move) while both prune_6 (α=0, removed outright) and dup_6 (α=2, −0.105) collapse it. A gentle damp is neither endpoint, so the 3-point map can't see it — it reads L6 as "touch it and it dies," when in fact a partial damp is the single best thing you can do there. L5 similarly peaks at α≈1.3.

This sorts the layers into two math populations: damp-down layers (L7/8/10/18/26/27 want α<0.5 — L26 is the "savant," optimum monotone toward zero, where a heavy partial damp ≈ pruning it without removal) and boost layers (L9/14/15/16/19/21/22/24/29/34 want α>1). Any per-layer sensitivity claim needs the dense single-pass α grid (~0.5–1.5), not the dup/prune endpoints.

The cleanest methodological result on the page On L6 the dup/prune endpoints and the dense α-grid disagree about the sign: prune says bad, dup says bad, a partial damp says best-on-the-board. A two-point instrument doesn't just lose precision here — it returns the wrong direction. That directly falsifies the dup/prune-endpoint map as a sensitivity instrument for non-monotone layers, which is most of the interesting ones.
Negative result — k-splitting does not raise the per-layer ceiling

The boost layers cap at ~α1.5, and the obvious move to push past it is to spread the gain across k damped passes. It fails: L22 and L34 each peak at single-pass α1.5, and spreading α>1.5 across k passes overshoots rather than extending the ceiling (sa22_S160_k6 d−0.022, sa34 d−0.07). The "k-split rescues α>1.5" hypothesis is refuted at these sites — stated plainly because it's the obvious thing to try.

§5Finding 4 — the Σα-law transferred, with the predicted downshift

Granite was the cross-model confirmation of the Σα-law first found on MiniCPM5. The governing variable for a stacked/damped layer is Σα = the sum of all per-pass alphas; k (copy count) scales the magnitude, Σα sets the sign. On Granite the peak sits at Σα ≈ 0.75 versus MiniCPM's ≈1.25 — and that downshift was named in advance: the pre-run note (2026-06-12, before the fair-battery run executed) flagged Granite's residual_multiplier = 0.22 as the suspect, and the run confirmed Σα≈0.75.

inf The working mechanism: a smaller per-layer residual write means a lower cumulative-α budget before the residual leaves the norm band. Beyond peak: ≈2.0 fading, ≥3 cratering, the same one-death-two-doors collapse. obs over-drive and negative-α both produce digit / special-token / CJK leak; inf residual-norm corruption is the proposed cause. dampall is neutral-to-negative on Granite (it isn't a collapsed model, so there's no pathology to relieve) — control behaviour consistent with MiniCPM's dampall "wins" having been de-collapse, not capability.

The strongest cross-model result the line has Two models, one shared variable, with a constant that moved in the direction and roughly the amount the architecture would predict. A prediction registered before the run, confirmed by it. It belongs on both pages with this shared figure.
