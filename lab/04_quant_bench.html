§1The table I couldn't find, and the flag that makes the RAM numbers comparable

If you run local models on a Mac, you've read the common recommendation to use MLX since it's native and faster. What I couldn't find anywhere was the table — runtime × quant method × batch size × {prefill, decode} × RAM, all measured in one equal environment on the same model. It doesn't exist online for a mundane reason: collecting it needs the same model across a dozen quant formats, three runtimes installed and warmed simultaneously, one harness measuring the same thing across all of them, and a machine nobody bothers to benchmark on — a base M4 Mini, 16 GB.

So I built it myself: one driver (unified_bench.py), three backend adapters (MLX in-process, llama.cpp via llama-server, PyTorch/MPS in-process), identical sweep grids and schema, and one RAM metric applied identically to all three. The model is MiniCPM5-1B (a plain 1.08B Llama, 24 layers): small enough to sweep exhaustively, architecturally vanilla enough that the results aren't a quirk of some exotic block.

Why benchmark at all Partly benchmarking for its own sake (the obligatory xkcd — and it has since paid off many times over on later sweeps). Mostly the frustration of never finding a definitive answer for the fastest way to sling tokens on a Mac mini, free of confounds like hardware, generation pathway, and runtime version.
llama.cpp mmaps its GGUF, and macOS phys_footprint excludes file-backed mapped pages — so a 640 MB model reads as ~0.10 GB of RAM, and any memory comparison against MLX is meaningless without correction. The fix is --no-mmap. Take one thing from this post for your own benchmarking: that flag, or your RAM numbers are fiction. The single methodological detail that silently breaks every naive comparison · §1

Throughput was quant-agnostic. At peak concurrency all MLX quants landed at ~1400–1670 tok/s (a ~15% spread) and all healthy llama quants at ~690–750. The M4 saturated on compute and bandwidth regardless of how the weights were packed. 2-bit, 4-bit, 8-bit, K-quants, IQ-quants — at batch, they converged. So throughput isn't a meaningful discriminator between quants in this run: pick on quality, RAM fit, and surgery-compatibility, not speed.

§3MLX's decode win is occupancy, not kernels

Why does MLX win batched decode if the quant doesn't matter? Not better arithmetic. Single-stream, MLX and llama are tied — same tok/s, same RAM. The gap opens only as you add concurrent streams: MLX keeps filling a bigger shared-KV batch (saturating around 192–256 streams) before it tops out, while in this run llama's continuous batcher plateaued earlier and lighter (64–128 streams — whether that's an architectural limit or a tuning artifact wasn't isolated). It's scheduler and allocator aggressiveness — MLX trades RAM for batch occupancy. That's the whole mechanism, or at least the simplest one consistent with what was observed.

The API hides most of it mlx_lm.batch_generate will not give you the 1670 ceiling — an O(width) Python sampler loop, Aho-Corasick stop machines, and a per-step .tolist() make it get slower as you widen (~214 tok/s @ width 256). The 1670 comes from a lean vectorized shared-cache loop (~1570 @ width 256). The stock API leaves 7–8× on the floor.

§4Throughput per gigabyte is what decides it on 16 GB

MLX buys that decode throughput with ~5–6× the RAM. At peak concurrency MLX sat at 10–12.7 GB — the only runtime that flirts with the M4's 14 GB Metal wired limit — while llama held 1.2–2.3 GB. On a 16 GB box, this, not tok/s, is the deciding factor, and it inverts the leaderboard.

Score by throughput per gigabyte — the figure of merit that matters when memory is the constraint, which on consumer Apple Silicon it always is — and llama.cpp dominates by 4–5×. Best llama (IQ2_XS) hit ~615 tok/s/GB; every llama quant (~490–615) beat every MLX quant (~114–120); PyTorch trailed at ~56. VERIFY MLX is the raw-speed-at-any-RAM-cost choice; llama.cpp is the efficient choice, and on a 16 GB machine efficiency is what runs.

RAM accounting caveat MLX's numbers are cumulative process high-watermarks; llama's are fresh-process per config. MLX RAM is somewhat overstated, llama mildly under-reported. The direction of the gap is real; treat the precise magnitude as an upper bound until a live Activity Monitor calibration is done.

§5PyTorch is last; prefill is compute-bound; the arithmetic floor

The fp16 fairness triad (identical weights, all three runtimes): MLX 1669 tok/s @ 256 streams / 12.45 GB; llama 778 @ 128 / 3.76 GB; PyTorch 368 @ 32 / 6.63 GB. PyTorch is ~4.5× behind MLX batched and ~3× behind llama, from eager/unfused-kernel overhead that persists even at matched precision. "Don't serve inference through HuggingFace on a Mac" appears to be correct, and now it has numbers — at least for this model and batch configuration. (Note: the IQ3_M llama row is EOS-broken here, total_tokens=1 — its 182 tok/s reading is garbage and is excluded throughout.)

Prefill was even more saturated than decode — ~2000 tok/s at pp512 with only ~12% spread across the whole quant ladder. Q4_0 tiles cleanest into Metal's GEMM and led; K-quants pay a block-structure tax and roll off after pp256; IQ types flatten or keep climbing. None of it was large enough to change a practical decision.

Why quant choice doesn't move the needle — the workload is pinned to a physical limit. Single-stream decode is memory-bandwidth-bound: a 4-bit 1.08B model is ~0.6 GB, so 120 GB/s ÷ 0.6 ≈ 200 tok/s; observed best ~155 = ~78% of that bound. 2-bit (~0.35 GB) predicts ~343, observed 218 ≈ 64%. Prefill is compute-bound: 2000 tok/s × ~2·1.08e9 FLOP/tok ≈ 4.3 TFLOP/s, in the M4 10-core GPU's fp16 range. Which, I suppose, answers the question I couldn't find online: for speed, quant choice wasn't a meaningful discriminator — pick the quant on quality.

§6Addendum — M5 Air vs M4 Mini, and the roofline

A cross-machine run (same model, same MLX/mlx-lm stack, the lean decode loop) on a borrowed fanless M5 MacBook Air against the M4 Mini: the M5 was ~2.3× across the board — decode (3700 vs 1615 peak tok/s), prefill (5350 vs 2200), wall (2700 vs 1162). That's far above the 1.27× memory-bandwidth ratio (153 vs 120 GB/s).

The leading hypothesis: the win is GPU compute and dispatch, not bandwidth. The ~200 tok/s ceiling is single-stream (bandwidth-bound); at batch width 64–384 decode appears compute-bound (weights reused across the batch, past the roofline knee), so it tracks prefill at ~2.3× — both riding the M5's ~2× GPU compute. No decode-specific magic; just which side of the roofline both kernels are on.

Stated as a prediction Figure 3 is an open experiment. Confirming the crossover needs sub-64 batch widths on both machines, and the M5 Air isn't currently accessible. The 3700 is also a burst number on a fanless chassis — whether it holds under 10–15 min sustained load is untested.
