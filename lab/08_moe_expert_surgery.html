Every other specimen in this lab treats a layer as the thing you operate on. Point the same machinery at a Mixture-of-Experts model and it stops working — and the way it stops working is informative enough to be its own result. This is the page where the line forks.

⌦ Correctionretired — for MoE
HypothesisA layer is the universal unit of surgery — duplicate, prune, or damp one and capability moves, on any architecture.
Why it looked trueIt held across every dense specimen — Granite, MiniCPM5 — where a layer genuinely is a unit of computation you can amplify.
Why it failedOn an MoE, duplicating a block re-runs the router and double-applies expert selection on an uncalibrated residual. The router sees an input distribution it was never trained on, null rates explode, strict scores floor. It's not "no effect" — it's "actively breaks the routing."
DispositionRetired for MoE. The unit of capacity here is the expert, not the layer — so the line forks to the expert axis. The rest of this page is the lever it forks to.

§1The wall

Whole-layer surgery has no purchase on a Mixture-of-Experts model, for two distinct reasons. Duplication corrupts routing — a duplicated MoE block is mechanically destructive, full stop. And pruning is the wrong axis, not harmful: pruning an MoE layer is an output-compliance fix — it unclogs a stalled output valve so the model emits a clean letter or number — not a reasoning fix.

Whether the unclogged output is correct depends entirely on whether the latent capability was already there. On factual multiple-choice (latent), unclogging surfaces a real answer the model knew — a genuine, large gain. On 16-digit arithmetic (not latent), unclogging just produces confident, well-formatted garbage: the strict phantom. Same mechanism, opposite verdict, decided by latent-knowledge presence — the exact compliance-not-capability pattern, here as the whole story rather than a footnote.

The reason is structural: on an MoE the unit of capacity is the expert — distributed across hundreds of them, ~8 active per token — not the layer. To touch what the model actually knows, you have to operate on experts.

The clean negative Came from Gemma 26B-A4B, a 30-layer MoE. "It failed the task" is at least two different failures — broken routing vs. an unclogged valve over absent capability — and only the architecture tells you which.

§2The expert-surgery toolkit

Three operations on the expert axis, three different jobs. REAP is a functional-importance cut — it measures each expert's data-conditioned contribution (gate weight × output, aggregated over a calibration set) and culls the low-saliency ones. That's a "what does the model actually use" cut, the opposite of the geometric cosine/delta scans the dense work uses.

TechniqueWhat it doesUse case
REAP · prunedelete low-utility experts (router-weighted activation pruning)shrink total params
ExpertClone · mergeblend similar expertsdensify, preserve knowledge
SEC · cloneoverwrite junk slots with copies of specialist expertsbias routing toward specialists, no retraining

REAP's published headline (Cerebras, arXiv 2510.13999) is 50% expert compression, near-lossless on code for very large MoEs (Qwen3-Coder-480B, Kimi-K2), with pruning beating merging on generative tasks because merging loses routing control. The lab's own datapoints below deliberately go deeper than that documented window, into the degraded-but-usable regime. The two concrete results that follow are an SEC replay and a REAP teardown; the lab's own runs of both are still ahead.

§3You can steal a clone map by diffing routers

Someone had surgically re-biased an MoE's routing toward its specialist experts, shipped the result, and withheld the how. xpressai published a modified MoE — the live card is Qwen3.6-35B-A3B-ExpertClone-Logic — but the implementation repo is not publicly accessible (and not on the Wayback Machine as of 2026-06), so the profiling methodology is permanently opaque. That opacity is exactly what raises the stakes of the router-diff method: without it, the surgery couldn't be recovered at all.

You don't need their code to replay the surgery, because of one architectural fact: the router tensor (ffn_gate_inp.weight) is F32 in every quant of the same base. In the cloned file, the victim router columns were overwritten with the donor's — so they now match the donor. Therefore a router column that matches base is a donor (untouched); a column that differs from base is a victim (overwritten). Donor→victim direction falls out definitively, no guessing.

Run that diff and you recover the clone map — 39 of 40 layers cleanly (layer 39 was genuinely untouched), with victim indices systematically small (0–17, the low-firing experts) and donors spread (1–229), which independently confirms a "lowest-total-firing expert as victim" selection rule. Then replay it: 39 × 4 = 156 mmap slab copies onto a runnable pre-quantized IQ2_M of the same base, near-instant, quant-agnostic. You've reproduced a gatekept model's surgery on hardware you own, without their code.

Honest scope This extracts and replays a published decision; it does not reverse-engineer the profiling step (what corpus, what criteria chose the donors). Building own clone maps from scratch is still future work — and the capability eval so far is preliminary, no fixed seed yet.
A strong clone doesn't visibly stumble on eyeball prompts — so the SEC delta is real-but-sub-perceptual, exactly where ad-hoc evaluation fails and the calibrated seed-paired battery earns its keep. The router-diff replay · §3 · capability eval still preliminary

§4Deep expert excision is usable below parity

You can't A/B a 100B→30B retention on a 16 GB Mac — you can't run the 100B base — so the locally-obtainable verdict is usability, not parity. And there's one clean, unconfounded datapoint, read straight off a published model's own card.

huihui-ai/Huihui4-8B-A4B is a REAP of gemma-4-26B-A4B-it: experts 128→32 (75% culled), 8 active/token, all 30 layers intact, a pure count-prune. The param math checks out exactly — base routed-expert 22.84B + fixed 2.40B = 25.23B; cull to 32 → routed 5.71B + unchanged 2.40B = 8.10B, dead-on the "8B" label. The 26→8B ratio is only 3.1× despite a 4× expert cut because fixed overhead (attention, dense FFN, embeddings, router, norms) doesn't scale with expert count.

The unconfounded number, from their own perplexity eval: straight 128→32 prune, 0 SFT steps, PPL 1.596 → 2.483 (+55%). Degraded, but not broken — 75% of the experts are gone and it's still a functioning model. The baseline that makes that interesting isn't the unpruned model — you can't run that on 16 GB — it's non-existence. The real comparison is "a degraded version you can actually run" vs. "nothing at all," which is why a +55% PPL hit reads as intriguing rather than disqualifying: this is an accessibility result, not a capability claim.

⌦ Grader-skepticism noteartifact, not result
The tempting readTheir post-SFT PPL drops below the 128-expert base (1.004 < 1.596) — so 32 experts beat 128?
Why it's falseThe SFT and the PPL eval are on the same 500 samples — eval-on-train-distribution leakage (memorising a narrow thinking-format), not genuine superiority.
The honest claimThe 0-step +55% number. The sub-base number is an artifact — and saying so is the difference between a benchmark and a press release.

§5Why this is the live direction for consumer ML

A 250B dense model is dead in the water locally (250 GB working set). A 250B MoE with mmap + expert offload has a working set of ~10 GB, because only the active experts are hot — tractable at ~1–3 tok/s for cold-NVMe pages. MoE + REAP + local is the one regime where a consumer machine gets to touch frontier-scale models at all.

The standing hypothesis (speculative — plausible mechanism, untested in this lab): active params, not total, may be the intelligence proxy, up to the trained router-k. Pruning total to 30–40B while keeping ~6–10B active per token might gain per-token quality — remaining experts get hit more often, the router is less stretched. The supporting datapoint, a 35→14B-A3B reaped model "nearly keeping up" with full siblings, is an eyeball read, not a paired eval — so this stays a hypothesis, not a finding. The honest tax: a reaped older base loses to a newer dense one (generational gains > REAP preservation), so the workflow only pays on current-SOTA bases and has to be re-run per base update.

The engine direction matches the rest of the lab's evolution: the refined surgery line is live-MLX-module patching, not GGUF slab-rewriting — which is also why ragged per-layer expert counts (the elegant "let a global saliency threshold decide each layer's count" version of REAP) are a dead end in stock GGUF (a single global n_expert scalar) and a natural fit in MLX.

Known limitations Confidencelow — this is a working log, not a results page
  • SEC capability is preliminary — extracted + replayed + vibe-tested, no fixed-seed paired battery run yet. The router-diff method is solid; the quantified recovery is the next step.
  • The lab's own REAP run has not happened — the plan is reaped-no-SFT first (build a deep prune, eval the pure prune, only train if compelling). The Huihui numbers are their teardown, read off the card.
  • Training on a 16 GB Mac is constrained — full fp16 SFT of an 8B ≈ 100 GB (impossible); QLoRA is CUDA-only (dead end on Apple Silicon); the only path is MLX-LM LoRA on a 4-bit quant, and where the adapters go to heal an MoE prune (router? surviving experts?) is unsettled.
  • All param / PPL / index numbers are point-in-time from memory — verify against the model cards and GGUF dumps before publishing. Fig 2's exact per-layer victim columns are illustrative; the clustering structure is the claim, not the specific indices.
