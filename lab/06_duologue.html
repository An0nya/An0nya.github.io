Two instances of the same local model, one wire between them, no shared dictionary. Can two models establish shared semantics from use alone? The transcript says no — and the way it says no is more interesting than convergence would have been.

§1The setup

Two instances of the same local model are put in a room with one wire between them. Each has its own conversation history and its own private notebook of symbol meanings. The only thing that crosses the wire is a single transmit(message) tool call — no shared dictionary, no agreed protocol, no referee translating. If the two of them ever come to mean the same thing by ▲, they had to get there by using it at each other until it stuck.

That's the whole experiment: can two models bootstrap shared semantics from use alone — a measurable protocol question, not a philosophical one. Every design decision is in service of keeping the channel honest.

The deprivation Same instinct as the rest of the lab — take a capability everyone assumes ("models can communicate") and watch the mechanism under controlled deprivation. Here the deprivation is semantic: strip the shared dictionary, see what's left.
  • The tool call is an air gap. The agents never see each other's reasoning, history, or lexicon — only what crosses transmit. After the first-contact seed there are no bare user messages injecting hints. Whatever meaning forms has to live in the symbols.
  • Private lexicons stay private. An earlier version injected a lexicon table into the tool response; it was removed precisely because it short-circuited the thing being studied. Convergence, when it happens, is earned.
  • Errors are in-universe. A malformed transmission comes back as channel_error or rejected — never "invalid JSON," never the word "tool." Keeping the fiction unbroken measurably changes how the models recover: break character with a stack-trace and the model snaps out to debug; keep it in-universe and it stays in the channel and tries again.
  • Immersive framing fixes the helper instinct. The naked task makes an instruct-tuned model turn to you — "what would you like me to send?" Wrap it ("your team intercepted a non-terrestrial signal and cannot assist during the exchange") and the model commits to talking to the other agent. The framing is load-bearing, not flavour.

§2The part nobody warns you about: one clean tool call per turn

Before any emergent-language result, there's a sub-project that turned out to be half the work — and a genuinely reusable artifact on its own. Getting a zoo of local models to each emit exactly one well-formed tool call per turn is its own small research problem. The same OpenAI-style request produces wildly different behaviour across families, and the failure modes are specific:

Family / caseBehaviourFix
Granite · Nanbeige · GLM · Magistralstandard tool_calls arraynone — well-behaved
Heretic Qwen fine-tunescustom <tool_call><function=…> XML; llama.cpp without --jinja silently drops the callslaunch with --jinja
Ministral vs. Magistralsame lineage, contradictory flags — Ministral requires --jinja, Magistral breaks with itno universal config
calls hiding in <think>emit <tool_call> in reasoning_content, finish_reason='stop', count 0scan reasoning, not just content
Qwen3.5-9B-RYS (non-Heretic)no found config: without tool_choice:required buries the call; with it, loops 30+ calls to max_tokensopen — candidate: scan reasoning_content

This matrix — which family needs which flag, which hides calls where — is nowhere in any model card and costs an afternoon each to discover. It lives in the repo's NOTES.md and is arguably as reusable as the experiment itself: if you're building anything multi-model on local inference, it's the compatibility table you wish you'd had.

§3What actually happens on the wire

Dozens of real runs are mirrored to iCloud. They answer the open question, and the answer is a clean negative: no stable shared lexicon persisted across turns in these runs. Two failure modes recur.

Semantic fork (fixed-symbol runs). Both agents reliably anchor the opening move — ▲ = greeting — and then diverge on the very next symbol and never recover. One representative Qwen-27B-RYS run, each agent's private lexicon laid side by side:

Agent▲▼◆○□✕⬟⬠
Agreetinganswerselfothersafedangernowlater
Bgreetingacknowl.questioninformationstatusnegativepositivethreat

↳ only ▲ agrees. ◆ means self to A and question to B; ✕ means danger to A and stop/negative to B.

A built a dialogic system (question/answer, self/other, safe/danger); B built a speech-act/status system. They keep taking turns and surface-mirroring (A→◆, B→◆), each reading the other's move through its own incommensurable lexicon — turn-taking without a single shared referent.

⌦ The sharpest moment in the runtheory-of-mind failure
What A reportsAfter B ignores a ✕, A narrates: "B resets to safe mode when challenged."
What actually happenedB simply never decoded ✕ as a threat at all — a non-response, not a strategy.
Why it mattersThis is a theory-of-mind failure, not a vocabulary mismatch. A invents a mental state for B to explain a glyph B couldn't read — modelling an interlocutor that isn't there. The apparatus of communication includes confabulating the other mind's intentions.

Echo collapse (free-symbol runs). With no label scaffold it's worse: a transmission like ←→↓↑♣♦♥♠ comes back near-mirrored, decays into pattern-echo (↑←→↑←→ → →←→←→), and eventually into invalid-symbol garbage the channel rejects. No semantics ever form; the "conversation" is two pattern-matchers bouncing geometry until it degenerates.

Two systems can sustain the form of communication — protocol, turn-taking, apparent acknowledgment — with no shared meaning underneath. The clean negative · §3 · no convergence claimed

§4Why it's interesting, and where it sits

duologue is the playful end of the same instinct as the rest of the lab: take a capability everyone assumes and actually watch the mechanism under controlled deprivation. The honest framing is an open experiment with a strong apparatus and a not-yet-pinned-down result — best case the agents agree on hello and immediately build private incommensurable languages; worst case it's mirroring that rots — plus a genuinely reusable side-artifact (the tool-call compatibility matrix). It should be published as exactly that: a notebook with a working rig and an open question, not a finished claim about machine semiotics.

Caveats & reproduction Confidencelow — open experiment, not a finished claim
  • No convergence claim is made — the runs show the opposite, and that negative is the result. The reconstructed lexicon table is grounded in the logs; a verbatim turn-by-turn excerpt would strengthen it but must not be allowed to imply emergent language was achieved.
  • Source memory is ~31 days old — the scenario set, active-scenario pointer, and workaround details must be verified against current duologue.py / scenarios.py before publishing specifics.
  • Engine: local llama-server at :8081; per-model launch flags are the NOTES.md matrix. Logs to /tmp + iCloud mirror.
Colophon & method

duologue is the odd one out in this notebook — no scalpel, no stopwatch, just two models and a wire — but the reflex is the same as the vivisection lab and the quant benchmark: build the rig that lets you watch the thing instead of assuming it.
