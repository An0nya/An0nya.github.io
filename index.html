Independent ML-evaluation · 2026

Local AI models, torn down, modified, and tested.

I'm a software engineer (professionally .NET, personally omni-curious) that's been trying out local LLMs, partially in hopes of finding a way to reduce my cloud usage, and partially in order to learn more about how they work by modifying them. You can learn a lot about how many things function by disassembling them, and LLMs are all the rage these days, which makes them a great candidate that won't end up in the landfill when I do something wrong.

The draw

I've been inspired by many people who spend their time and prodigious compute on fine-tuning, quantizing, and experimenting with models, and all the open source software they publish. I have no pretensions of creating models that can compete or eclipse those released by the big organizations, but I can attempt new experiments and share what I've come across for others to read.

Another draw is the feel of hanging out at the raw edge of technological progress in the community driven space of local LLMs. It resembles to me past technology boom periods where anyone could become a wizard in a new technology and people cobbled together enterprise hardware to host free websites or services. For my part, I'm doing all this on a base model Mac mini, which admittedly is uniquely capable for LLMs, but still nothing on the scale of the compute available to frontier companies or even research groups. The ability to perform all this on my own device, no signups, no fees, and no worrying that a tweak I've tried will cost me wasted money on a rented server. All this runs in the background and I can pivot my tasks when I want.

Intervention

Exploring what happens when you modify models. Layers duplicated, pruned, damped, and recombined, then measured to see how the resulting model fares.

The model layer experiments are inspired by the work of David Ng, and while I haven't had the same amount of success on leaderboards, I have been expanding on his method and tackling the ever present difficulty of evaluating model performance.

Gemma 4 31B — the heavyweight

arch
dense · 31B
runtime
llama.cpp · IQ2_M
op
dup/prune sweep · 176 cfg

This is a report of a 176-config dup/prune sweep of a flagship 31B model squeezed into 16 GB at IQ2_M, and not one surgery won. This model stressed my machine and my patience, but given its size I had hoped to finally see a notable result. The original method suggests that a model with more layers develops a real language-independent section of layers that could be duplicated to improve reasoning without disturbing its processing. These findings suggest minimal to no improvement.

Results: It's robust to a single cut but rigid under a structural one, with damage scaling as span × depth. The seed-noise lived at the front of the stack, not the back. Realistically too large of a model to attempt this on, and while the improved instrumentation might deliver more honest results, I'm leaving this one at the preliminary finding to spare myself.

HRM — a promising idea, not model

arch

runtime
MLX · bf16
op
loop depth · recurrence
status
draft

A Hierarchical Recurrent Model sounds like the perfect experimentation platform for me. After all, my whole hypothesis is increasing reasoning depth through looping layers rather than just making a bigger model or training it. This is a novel class of transformer LLMs that's not ready for prime time yet, but that didn't stop me from exploring the exact promise of looped transformers - a built in system to multiply the number of passes the token takes through layers. The first overnight test delivered outstanding and exciting results, but as is a recurring theme here, upon further inspection and more rigorous testing, it turned out to be a fluke.

Results: greedy sampling and no replication manufactured a false champion. The promise of simply increasing the number of loops hit a wall when faced with a larger, general-purpose model than the original paper.

Observation

The other side of layer edits is the hidden layer scanning that led to the hypothesis behind RYS. David Ng showed cosine similarity charts for paired phrases, layer by layer, that illustrated the potential of a “reasoning zone.” It's a powerful visualization that demonstrates how the model transforms all inputs into intermediate values that don't correspond to our language or understanding, and I enjoy seeing these visualizations for each of the models.

This brand-new dense Gemma model dropped right as I was refining the MLX hidden-state extraction script, and it seemed like a perfect opportunity to crack the model open before even a single fine-tune came out. The scan was interesting, but I took it further by investigating per-token hidden states - comparing two sentences that differ by barely a word but whose meanings diverge widely. This created a visual demonstration of where in the model a token takes on meaning, rather than being a number corresponding to a dictionary entry.

Finding: the probes converge on one concept zone at layers 18-27; the final layer spends it on next-token prediction. Per-token, a word's sense resolves in that same band: the model fixes "crane" as the bird before the sentence ends.

Method

The recurring pattern in all the model sweeps I've performed is just how crucial the methodology of evaluating the model is. It comes down to crafting or importing the right question set to choose probes, refining a grading pattern to reduce false positives and false negatives, setting up the inference runtime to restrict the model from CoT reasoning, tweaking the prompt to encourage it to respond with just the answer, playing with the chat template, etc. - and then doing all that over again on a new model that plays by different rules.

On top of that, when you're experimenting with LLMs on consumer hardware, every tweak that makes it run faster is worth it, from benchmarking runtimes to making a custom prompt-batching pipeline to reduce overhead. Even step one - scanning the model to assess roughly what layers will be actionable - can be optimized.

The hidden-state extraction script was written for PyTorch with raw fp16 transformers, but I needed it to work with quantized models. And then I investigated whether MLX could perform the same task, since it also operates on raw transformers rather than a packaged GGUF. Editing layers on the fly in MLX is natively supported, but digging into this functionality offered the opportunity to try out layer damping, which turned out to be a useful layer-modification path, less disruptive than pruning or duplicating. It also set up future experiments in related tangents - activation steering/injection, sparse autoencoders, and diffusion models.

The terse Methods & references page holds the shared vocabulary; the vivisection & vivarium overview-methodology pages will join it.

Methods · measurement

Anatomy of a sweep

Running the surgery isn't difficult once you establish the mechanics of it; extracting and evaluating the resulting model's response is where it turns into work. Three traps do most of the damage: a lenient grader inflates partial credit, the model can echo the answer straight back, and format-compliance passes for capability if you don't check. So before spending compute I profile how much headroom the baseline even leaves to measure, then calibrate the grading channel against it.

Method: most false wins come from the measurement, not the model, so the grader gets calibrated before any compute is spent.

Runtime · benchmark

MLX is the fastest local runtime*

The asterisk is the whole point: 1B dense model, batched decode only, M4 Mini, custom generation loop, raw tok/s - every qualifier is on the label. The more interesting result, outside that narrow use case, is that MLX and llama.cpp are pretty evenly matched, so there isn't really a headline about which runtime is better.

Finding: "fastest" is true for batched decode, where MLX roughly doubles llama.cpp's throughput at the expense of more than double the RAM. For single-prompt processing, the runtimes and most quants are comparable. Only PyTorch is the slow outlier on Apple Silicon.

Orchestration · emergent comm

duologue - one model, two agents

Duologue is more in the spirit of the model experimentation in the previous sections than a functional use for agents, but it did submerge me into the tricky world of context management and orchestration. It started with two models in RAM concurrently. I can't run two Qwen-27Bs at once, though, so the trick became feeding a single model a carefully crafted history that makes it believe it's talking to someone else. That resolved the mechanistic aspect. The fun part ended up being setting up various scenarios for it: first to see whether it recognizes the other agent as AI, and later whether the two agents could communicate under strict restrictions on their messages to each other.

Finding: When they aren't flopping tool calls, the models do attempt to establish communication with the limited vocabulary, but none of the local models managed to achieve shared understanding.

Systems · ETL pipeline

mem0-processor

The project that kicked off everything else here. I wanted a persistent memory store kept current by LLMs, but the options were flawed: claude.ai's user memories are occasionally wrong, Claude Code has the model write its own unreliably, the two don't even talk to each other, and everything I found leaned on paid Claude to generate the memories. So I built a pipeline that uses free open-weight models to summarize my sessions and upload them to mem0, keeping close control over the transcript and prompt. Trusting those local summaries meant scoring the models to see which were any good, and that scoring rig quietly became a 60+ model evaluation harness.

Finding: scoring each summary against hand-written ground-truth fact sets and ranking by z-score is what lets "this model summarizes better than that one" actually mean something.

Systems · agent memory

Teaching an agent to read its own logs

What do you do when you have 1200 session summaries from hundreds of different local models sitting on your storage drive from testing the mem0 pipeline? I couldn't let that go to waste, and I'd been meaning to optimize my CLAUDE.md empirically from past experience. The summaries are meant to be small enough to parse easily, but cumulatively they add up to a corpus too large for any model to process at once. Through several layers of aggregation, I used Claude to distill them into meaningful observations that measurably improved my system prompt and memory system, to the point where starting a new session can feel frictionless.

Finding: I weight what recurs, not what was loudest; that's the whole design decision.

How I work

As evidenced by the variety of topics above, my attention jumps around when unbound, but the one thing I'm strict on is keeping my results in check. I don't claim a model that got better. I claim a better measurement layer under it: seed discipline, paired effect sizes, and a strict regrade that culls the wins that don't replicate. I direct and decide; large models read code, run the statistics, and draft. The judgment about what's true is mine. I work collaboratively with Claude to accelerate my workflow and to explore novel areas I wouldn't have the faintest clue where to start.

Traceability

Every number on this site traces back to a file in a repo: the config hash lives in the filename, the raw per-probe results are kept as JSONL, and the strict score always leads. I won't publish a comparison that isn't backed by an artifact you could open and re-run yourself.

Transparency

Many of the headline findings on this page were wrong, and were wrong due to incorrect interpretation or configuration. I want to ensure that I don't overstate my results, and that I provide the full story of how I arrived at them rather than cherry-picking or stripping out the failures.

Efficiency

I appreciate efficiency in software engineering, both from a performance aspect and the perspective of selecting the right framework to match the needs of a project. This is reflected in this site: it's static HTML, ~1.5 KB of JS, slim SVG figures, no framework, one cookieless tracker. It is everything it needs to be and nothing more, and the simple structure has the added benefit of being easily parseable by screen readers or LLMs, and treats accessibility as a priority, not an afterthought.
